Instructions

You are about to be presented with 500 Steam discussion posts published before the named game was released. Your task is not to decide directly whether a post is PRUS. Instead, you will record the observable components from which PRUS can be determined.

What is PRUS?

Pre-Release User Speculation (PRUS) is the user-to-user expression and exchange of low-commitment conjectural propositions about an unreleased product, produced under conditions, and in service of making sense, of incomplete product information.

Simply put:

PRUS occurs when a user expresses uncertainty about an idea concerning an unreleased game or the experience surrounding it. In this exercise, that idea is identified through three components rather than through a direct PRUS judgement.

How to Code Each Post:

Read the title and full displayed post as one connected contribution. First identify whether the post expresses uncertainty. If it does, identify whether that uncertainty applies to a recoverable claim, explanation, prediction, interpretation, theory, hypothetical, or unresolved alternative. If both components are present, select every product topic domain covered by the uncertain idea, or select No qualifying product topic domain. One sentence may supply all the components, or nearby statements may work together.

THE THREE COMPONENTS

Code the components in this order. The site will derive PRUS afterward.

1

Uncertainty cue or framing

Does the post contain wording or framing that presents something as possible, tentative, inferred, hypothetical, unresolved, unverified, assumed, guessed, or not fully certain? A question mark is not automatically an uncertainty cue. Hopes, suggestions, assumptions, conditionals, and implied possibilities can provide uncertainty framing even without words such as “may” or “might.”

2

Uncertain proposition

Does that uncertainty apply to an identifiable claim, explanation, prediction, interpretation, theory, hypothetical, or unresolved alternative? The uncertainty cannot exist in isolation; it must be uncertainty about something.

3

Product topic domain

Is the uncertain proposition about the game’s content, performance, or requirements/access? Select every applicable domain. If it concerns none of them, record that explicitly rather than forcing a domain.

A post is derived as containing PRUS only when all three components are present. The pre-release and user-to-user conditions are already fixed by the sample and therefore are not separate coding decisions.

Examples

MOST COMMON EXAMPLE TYPES

The following examples show how the three components apply to common forms of pre-release discussion. The labels show the coding sequence rather than asking for a direct PRUS decision.

Example type 1

Claims, explanations, predictions, and interpretations

These cases show the difference between an uncertain proposition and the same subject matter stated as a firm fact or opinion.

1 YES · 2 YES · CONTENT

A possible explanation for something shown before release.

“The locked building probably becomes a player base later.”

1 YES · 2 YES · CONTENT

A prediction presented with reduced certainty.

“With a map that large, matches might take longer than the trailer suggests.”

1 YES · 2 YES · CONTENT

An interpretation that leaves alternatives unresolved.

“Either the final game has another region, or that rail line is only decorative.”

1 NO · STOP

A future fact stated without uncertainty.

“The game launches next Friday.”

1 NO · STOP

A direct opinion that does not advance an uncertain idea.

“The combat shown in the trailer looks boring.”

1 YES · 2 YES · NO DOMAIN

An uncertain idea that is not about a qualifying product topic domain.

“The studio manager might be tired after answering all these forum posts.”

Example type 2

Questions, requests, hopes, and suggestions

These forms can express uncertainty about a proposition, but their grammatical form alone does not satisfy either component. A question counts only when it raises or implies an unresolved possibility about the product or the wider product experience.

1 YES · 2 YES · CONTENT

A question that directly raises a possible product state.

“Could the locked area be another map in the full game?”

1 YES · 2 YES · CONTENT

A question that is phrased as a request but advances an explanation.

“Why show three empty class slots unless more classes are planned?”

1 YES · 2 YES · CONTENT

A hope that also introduces an uncertain product possibility.

“I hope the empty slot means another class is coming at launch.”

1 YES · 2 YES · PERFORMANCE

A suggestion that includes an uncertain consequence.

“They should improve the servers because they may struggle with the launch crowd.”

1 NO · STOP

A question seeking only a currently available fact or fix.

“My controller isn’t working. Which setting fixes it?”

1 NO · STOP

A pure request or preference without an uncertain product idea.

“Please add split-screen.”

1 NO · STOP

A question that asks only for a currently knowable release detail.

“Which edition includes early access?”

When determining whether a question contains PRUS, disregard its question form and ask: does the post advance or endorse a possible answer, explanation, prediction, interpretation, or future product state? If not, it is information-seeking and should be classified as Not PRUS. A question seeking only a currently available fact, instruction, or immediate fix does not qualify merely because the answer is unknown to the writer. A hope, suggestion, assumption, or conditional can still provide uncertainty framing when it introduces or implies a possible product state or outcome.

Example type 3

Demos, betas, and Early Access

Current experiences with a pre-release build can supply evidence for an uncertain interpretation of the wider product. A bare report of what happened is not enough.

1 YES · 2 YES · PERFORMANCE

A current experience used to infer a possible release-state problem.

“The demo keeps crashing, so the launch build may have the same issue.”

1 YES · 2 YES · CONTENT

A current experience that raises a possibility about the wider game.

“The combat feels limited in the demo. Is this all there will be?”

1 YES · 2 YES · PERFORMANCE

A concern about how a pre-release problem may develop.

“The beta performance is rough. I’m not sure they can fix it before launch.”

1 NO · STOP

A direct report of the current build without an uncertain interpretation.

“The demo crashed twice today.”

1 NO · STOP

A resolved technical problem.

“The demo crashed because my graphics driver was outdated. Updating it fixed the problem.”

A pre-release problem supplies the components when it is used to raise or imply an unresolved proposition about the game or product experience. A current-state report that stops at what happened, or explains and resolves the problem, does not.

Component-coding boundaries

  • A direct factual claim, even about the future, does not provide an uncertainty cue.
  • Ordinary excitement, dislike, praise, criticism, sarcasm, or negation does not provide an uncertainty cue by itself.
  • Advice, obligation, permission, or ability is not uncertainty unless it also reduces commitment to a proposition.
  • “I’m not sure” supplies uncertainty, but without an identifiable uncertain idea it does not supply Component 2.
  • For a question, disregard its question form and look for a possible answer, explanation, prediction, interpretation, or future product state advanced or endorsed by the post. Without one, the question is information-seeking and is coded No.
  • Hopes, suggestions, assumptions, and conditional statements can supply uncertainty framing when they introduce or imply a possible product state, even without an explicit modal cue.
  • Resolved technical explanations and unrelated discussion do not satisfy the component sequence.

Classification

PRODUCT TOPIC DOMAINS

After identifying both uncertainty and an uncertain proposition, select every product topic domain covered by that proposition. A single post may involve more than one. If the uncertain proposition concerns none of the three domains, select No qualifying product topic domain.

Content

Speculation about story, mechanics, characters, modes, maps, features, monetization, or gameplay experience.

Example: "The demo probably hints at a bigger crafting system."

Performance

Speculation about technical quality, optimization, FPS, bugs, crashes, servers, or launch stability.

Example: "It might run badly on older GPUs at launch."

Requirements / Access

Speculation about platform access, hardware specs, storage, regions, editions, release timing, or play conditions.

Example: "The full version may need more RAM than the demo."
